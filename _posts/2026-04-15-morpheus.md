## What it is

The model is a discrete masked diffusion architecture that generates drug-like molecules. You can steer it with a number to maximise (like drug-likeness) or a natural language description like "soluble molecule with a pyridine ring, molecular weight under 400."

We built one modular architecture and tested it at three scales: 4.4M parameters on a laptop, 27M for text conditioning, and 150M on a GPU cluster (ongoing).

---

## Why discrete diffusion

Most molecule generation models use SMILES strings, where random text usually decodes to a chemically invalid molecule. We use **SELFIES** instead: a representation where any string is guaranteed to produce a valid molecule.

Paired with discrete masked diffusion (MaskGIT-style), the model learns by masking random tokens in a molecule and predicting the originals. At generation time it starts from a fully masked sequence and iteratively fills in the most confident positions first, like solving a crossword puzzle.

The result: **100% chemical validity by construction**, no correction step needed. Our closest comparison (TGM-DLM, AAAI 2024, 180M parameters) only reaches 87.1% validity even with a dedicated correction network.

---

## RL for property optimisation

We used REINFORCE to push the model toward more drug-like molecules, measured by QED (a score from 0 to 1 combining molecular weight, solubility, and other properties).

The standard approach failed completely. Generating a molecule, scoring it, then computing log-probability in a single forward pass collapsed QED from 0.742 to 0.38. The problem: MaskGIT generation is 50 iterative steps, but the single-pass evaluation sees none of that process. The gradient flows through the wrong computation.

The fix was to instrument the generation loop: record each token's log-probability at the step it gets committed, then sum across all 50 steps. The gradient now flows through the actual generation process. QED improved from **0.742 to 0.837, a 12.8% gain**.

This is the same principle as DDPO (Berkeley, 2023) for image diffusion. We arrived at it through debugging, then found the connection.

**Reward shaping.** QED-only training narrowed structural diversity: scaffold variety dropped from 95% to 89%. Adding a molecular weight term and a diversity penalty recovered variety while keeping the property gain.

## Natural language conditioning

RL optimises a single number. Real drug design needs richer control. We extended the architecture with cross-attention layers connecting a frozen SciBERT encoder to the transformer, then fine-tuned on ChEBI-20, a public dataset of 26,000 molecule-description pairs.

Key finding: **domain-specific encoders beat generic ones regardless of size.** SciBERT (768-dimensional, chemistry-pretrained) consistently outperformed BGE-large (1024-dimensional, general English) across all metrics.

The systematic ablation, each step motivated by the result before it:

| Configuration | Morgan Tanimoto |
|---|---|
| BGE frozen (baseline) | 0.159 |
| + EOS fix | 0.199 |
| + Contrastive BGE | 0.239 |
| + Switch to SciBERT | 0.251 |
| + Train 20 epochs | 0.299 |
| + Inference reranking (N=10) | 0.310 |

## The hard part: ring topology

The model's biggest weakness is ring-containing molecules. Morgan Tanimoto (the strictest fingerprint metric) drops from 0.451 on chain molecules to 0.200 on polycyclic ones, a 2.26x gap.

The cause is architectural. In SELFIES, ring closures use a count token that says "bond back N atoms." Predicting that count requires knowing what atoms appear between the closure and its target, but in parallel decoding those positions may still be masked. Ring count accuracy degrades from 96% on acyclic molecules to 0% on molecules with six or more rings.

We're investigating block diffusion (predicting positions in groups so context is resolved before dependent tokens are predicted) as a potential fix.

---

## What we learned

1. **Per-step RL gradients are necessary for diffusion.** The naive single-pass proxy fails completely. This matches DDPO for continuous diffusion.
2. **Domain-specific encoders beat larger generic ones.** Smaller SciBERT outperformed larger BGE across every metric.
3. **Representation sets the ceiling, not parameter count.** The remaining gap to TGM-DLM is discrete tokens vs. continuous embeddings, not model size.
4. **Modularity pays off.** We swapped encoders three times and added RL to the same backbone without retraining from scratch.

## What's next

The RL and text conditioning components exist independently on the same backbone. The natural next step is combining them: generate a molecule that scores high on a disease-specific activity classifier while satisfying a text description. The 150M scaling study is also ongoing.
